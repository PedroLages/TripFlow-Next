## Shanghai (Aug 27-30)

### Tea

Chinese tea makes an excellent souvenir -- lightweight, packable, and genuinely better bought at the source. The two best teas to buy in Shanghai are **longjing** (Dragon Well green tea, native to nearby Hangzhou) and **tieguanyin** (Iron Goddess oolong from Fujian).

**Where to buy:**

| Shop / Market | Location | What to Expect | Price Range (USD) |
|---------------|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Tianshan Tea City** | 520 Zhongshan West Rd, Changning (near Yan'an W Rd) | Largest tea market in Shanghai. Hundreds of vendors across multiple floors. Serious buyers come here. Taste before buying. | Longjing: $7-40/100g depending on grade; Tieguanyin: $4-25/100g |
| **Laoximen Tea Market** | Near Laoximen Station, Old City | Smaller, more local-feeling. Less English spoken but authentic. | Similar to Tianshan but slightly cheaper |
| **Nayuki or HeyTea shops** | Various (malls, French Concession) | Modern tea brands -- good for packaged gift sets, not loose leaf | $5-15 per gift box |

**How to buy tea without getting scammed:**

- Always taste before buying. Legitimate shops will brew samples freely -- this is standard practice, not a sales tactic.
- Never follow strangers who invite you to a "tea ceremony" or "tea house" (see Scam Awareness section below).
- Bring your own reference price: decent longjing runs $10-20/100g; anything over $40/100g should be exceptional quality.
- Buy in vacuum-sealed bags for freshness on the journey home.
- The more a vendor pushes a specific expensive tea, the more skeptical you should be.

### Silk and Custom Tailoring

**South Bund Fabric Market (Lujiabang Road Fabric Market)**

- **Address**: 399 Lujiabang Road, Huangpu District (Metro: Line 8/9, Lujiabang Road Station)
- **What**: Multi-story market with hundreds of fabric stalls and in-house tailors who can make custom suits, dresses, shirts, coats, and cheongsam (qipao).
- **Turnaround**: Most tailors can complete a garment in 24-48 hours with one fitting. Plan your visit on Day 1 (Aug 27) so you can pick up on Day 2 or 3.
- **Quality varies widely** -- ask to see finished samples, check seam quality, and negotiate firmly.

| Item | Price Range (USD) | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Custom men's suit (2-piece) | $60-200 | Cashmere blend at the high end |
| Custom dress shirt | $15-40 | Get 2-3 for a better per-unit price |
| Custom dress / cheongsam | $40-150 | Silk cheongsam at the high end |
| Women's tailored blazer | $40-100 | |
| Cashmere scarf | $15-30 | Good gift; negotiate for multiple |
| Raw silk fabric (per meter) | $5-15 | |

**Tips**: Start at the upper floors where there is less foot traffic and vendors are more willing to negotiate. Always get a fitting before final pickup. Bring a photo of the style you want.

### Souvenirs

**Tianzifang**
- **Location**: Lane 210, Taikang Road, French Concession (Metro: Line 9, Dapuqiao Station)
- Narrow alleyways converted into boutique shops, studios, and cafes
- Look for: hand-painted Shanghai postcards, Chinese calligraphy art, silk accessories, quirky local-designer goods
- Prices are fixed in most boutiques here. Budget $5-30 per item.

**Dongtai Road Area (former Antiques Market)**
- The original open-air market was relocated in 2015, but the surrounding streets still have antique and curio shops
- Look for: propaganda posters ($5-20 for reproductions), Mao-era memorabilia, vintage Shanghai advertising art, old coins
- Reproductions are openly sold and clearly labeled at honest shops -- if someone insists something is a genuine antique at a low price, it is not

**Shanghai Propaganda Poster Art Centre**
- **Address**: Basement, Block B, 868 Huashan Road (buzzer entry -- call ahead or check their WeChat)
- Genuine vintage propaganda posters from the 1950s-1970s. A fascinating private collection with items for sale.
- Originals: $50-500+; reproduction prints: $10-30

## Hong Kong (Aug 30 - Sep 2)

### Electronics

Hong Kong's reputation as an electronics bargain paradise has faded. Prices for major brands (Apple, Samsung, Sony) are now comparable to or occasionally higher than US/EU prices after accounting for exchange rates. That said, some niche items and accessories can still be cheaper.

**Where to look:**

| Area | What to Find | Vibe |
|------|-------------|------|
| **Sham Shui Po** (Metro: Sham Shui Po Station) | Budget electronics, adapters, cables, phone accessories, vintage audio gear, SIM cards | Gritty, local, authentic. Best for small parts and accessories. Apliu Street is the main drag. |
| **Mong Kok (Sim City)** | Phones, phone cases, screen protectors | Multi-floor mall of small phone vendors. Compare prices between stalls. |
| **Wan Chai Computer Centre** | Laptops, desktop parts, peripherals | Small but packed. Good for PC builders. |
| **Fortress / Broadway** (chain stores) | Major brand electronics | Fixed prices, reliable warranties. Good baseline for price comparison. |

**Tips**: Always compare with your home country prices on Amazon or similar before buying. Dual-SIM phones and certain Android models may be cheaper. Warranty is usually Hong Kong only -- not international.

**Fa Yuen Street ("Sneaker Street"), Mong Kok**

- Brands: Nike, Adidas, New Balance, Asics, Converse, Vans, plus local Hong Kong brands

- **Streetwear**: CLOT (Edison Chen's brand) and MADNESS have Hong Kong flagship stores; also check I.T (multi-brand HK fashion chain) for curated streetwear

### Souvenirs

**G.O.D. (Goods of Desire)**
- **Locations**: Hollywood Road (Central), Causeway Bay, and others
- Hong Kong's most iconic local design brand. Products feature classic HK imagery: mahjong tiles, dim sum motifs, old-building facades, Cantonese slang
- Great for: tote bags ($10-20), cushion covers ($15-30), kitchenware ($5-25), clothing ($20-50)
- Everything is well-designed and feels distinctly Hong Kong -- far better than generic tourist souvenirs

**PMQ (Former Police Married Quarters)**
- **Location**: 35 Aberdeen Street, Central
- Converted heritage building now housing 100+ local designer studios and shops
- Find handmade jewelry, ceramics, leather goods, printed textiles from independent Hong Kong artists
- Prices: $10-80 depending on the item and artist

## Osaka (Sep 2-5)

### Kitchen Knives

Osaka is one of the best cities in the world to buy Japanese kitchen knives. The city has a centuries-old tradition of blade-making.

**Doguyasuji (Kitchen Street)**
- **Location**: Namba area, between Namba Station and Nipponbashi (just south of Dotonbori)
- A covered arcade street dedicated entirely to kitchen tools: knives, pots, ceramics, restaurant supplies
- Roughly 10-15 knife shops in a short stretch

**What to know before buying:**

| Knife Type | Use | Price Range (USD) | Notes |
|-----------|-----|-------------------|-------|
| **Santoku** | General-purpose, the Japanese equivalent of a chef's knife | $30-200 | Best first Japanese knife |
| **Gyuto** | Japanese chef's knife (Western-style blade shape) | $50-300 | Versatile, great for home cooks |
| **Nakiri** | Vegetable knife, flat blade | $30-150 | Excellent for precision vegetable work |
| **Petty** | Small utility knife | $20-100 | Good gift, easy to pack |
| **Deba** | Fish butchering | $40-200 | Specialized; only if you break down fish |
| **Yanagiba** | Sashimi slicing | $60-400+ | Single-bevel, requires skill to maintain |

**Key terms:**
- **Stainless steel (e.g., VG-10)**: Lower maintenance, does not rust easily. Good for most home cooks.
- **Carbon steel (e.g., White #2, Blue #2)**: Sharper edge, easier to sharpen, but rusts if not dried immediately. For serious cooks.
- **Damascus / Suminagashi**: Layered steel with a wavy pattern. Aesthetic; the pattern does not affect performance.
- **Honyaki**: Single-steel, hand-forged. Expensive ($200+), for collectors and professionals.

**Recommended shops on Doguyasuji:**

| Shop | Specialty | Price Range | English? |
|------|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Tower Knives Osaka** | Wide range, English-speaking staff, knife engraving | $50-300 | Yes -- specifically caters to tourists |
| **Sakai Takayuki** | High-end Sakai-forged blades | $80-500+ | Some English |
| **Doguyasuji shops (various)** | Mid-range daily-use knives | $30-150 | Limited |

**Tips**: If you want engraving (your name in Japanese characters), Tower Knives does it for free with purchase. Bring your knife in your checked luggage -- do not attempt to carry it on the plane.

### Don Quijote (Donki)

Don Quijote is a Japanese discount chain that is open late (many locations are 24 hours) and sells everything from snacks to cosmetics to electronics to costumes. It is chaotic, overstocked, and genuinely fun to browse. The Dotonbori location in Osaka is massive.

**Best things to buy at Donki:**

| Category | Items | Price Range (USD) |
|----------|-------|-------------------|
| **Kit-Kat flavors** | Matcha, strawberry cheesecake, sake, melon, sweet potato -- Japan has 300+ flavors | $3-8 per box |
| **Beauty / skincare** | Shiseido Senka face wash, Biore sunscreen, LuLuLun face masks, Canmake cosmetics | $3-15 |
| **Medicine** | Salonpas pain patches, Cabbage enzyme stomach pills, eye drops (Sante FX) | $3-10 |
| **Snacks** | Tokyo Banana (seasonal), Pocky, Hi-Chew, rice crackers, instant ramen variety packs | $2-8 |
| **Household** | Japanese thermos (Zojirushi), heated eye masks (Kao MegRhythm) | $10-40 |

**Tax-free**: Donki participates in the tax-free program (see General Tips section). Spend over 5,000 yen (~$33) in a single transaction and show your passport at the tax-free counter for a 10% discount.

### Vintage and Secondhand Clothing

**Amerikamura (American Village)**
- **Location**: Shinsaibashi area, central Osaka (Metro: Shinsaibashi Station)
- Osaka's hub for youth culture, thrift stores, and vintage fashion

- Prices: $5-40 for most items. Vintage Levi's and rare band tees can run higher.
- Also check: **Kinji** (large chain vintage store) and **2nd Street** (secondhand chain with quality control)

## Kyoto (Sep 5-9)

### Traditional Crafts

Kyoto has been Japan's cultural capital for over a thousand years, and its traditional crafts reflect that heritage. These are among the best souvenirs you can buy in Japan.

**Kiyomizu-yaki Pottery**
- Kyoto's signature ceramic style, produced near Kiyomizu-dera temple since the 17th century
- Range from simple tea cups ($10-20) to elaborate hand-painted vases ($100+)
- **Where**: Shops lining Higashiyama streets (Ninenzaka, Sannenzaka) leading to Kiyomizu-dera; also Gojozaka (Pottery Street)
- **Touan** on Gojozaka is a well-regarded workshop with a showroom and reasonable prices

**Nishijin-ori Textiles**
- Kyoto's traditional silk weaving, used in kimono obi (sashes), purses, and accessories
- **Nishijin Textile Center** (Imadegawa area): museum and shop with woven accessories like coin purses ($10-20), neckties ($20-40), and fabric panels
- Full obi sashes start at $100+ and are collector's items

**Washi Paper**
- Handmade Japanese paper used for calligraphy, origami, bookbinding, and decorative purposes
- **Kyukyodo** (Teramachi Street, downtown): est. 1663, sells washi paper, incense, calligraphy supplies, and beautifully bound notebooks ($5-25)
- **Wagami no Mise** (near Nishiki Market): wide selection of decorative washi sheets ($2-5 each)

**Incense**
- Kyoto is Japan's incense capital. Two world-renowned incense houses:

| Shop | Location | Founded | What to Buy | Price Range (USD) |
|------|----------|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Shoyeido** | Karasuma-Nijo (main shop); also branches near temples | 1705 | Stick incense sets, sachets, incense holders | $5-30 for gift sets; $10-50 for premium lines |
| **Kungyokudo** | Horikawa-Nijo, near Nijo Castle | 1594 | Traditional incense, incense bags (nioi-bukuro), scented paper | $3-20 for small items; $20-60 for curated sets |

Both shops offer beautifully packaged gift sets that are lightweight and easy to transport. Excellent gifts.

### Matcha and Sweets

**Ippodo Tea**
- **Location**: Teramachi Street, downtown Kyoto (also a branch in Tokyo's Marunouchi)
- Est. 1717. One of the most respected tea houses in Japan.
- Buy: ceremonial-grade matcha ($15-40 for a 20-40g tin), genmaicha, hojicha, gyokuro
- They have an in-store tasting room (Kaboku Tearoom) where you can try before buying
- Staff speak English and will help you choose based on your preferences

**Tsujiri**
- **Location**: Uji (a short train ride from Kyoto, the birthplace of Japanese matcha) and branches on Shijo Street
- Known for matcha sweets: soft serve, parfaits, and packaged matcha confections for souvenirs ($5-15)

**Nakamura Tokichi**
- Another legendary Uji tea house with a Kyoto Station branch
- Matcha jelly, matcha soba, and packaged tea gift sets ($10-30)

**Wagashi (Traditional Japanese Sweets)**
- Beautiful, seasonal confections -- too delicate to pack home, but worth buying to eat during your stay
- **Kagizen Yoshifusa** (Gion): est. 1855, kuzu-kiri (arrowroot noodles) and seasonal wagashi
- **Tsuruya Yoshinobu** (Imadegawa): elegant wagashi with a counter where you watch confections being made

### Nishiki Market

**Location**: Nishiki-koji Street, downtown Kyoto (Metro: Shijo Station or Karasuma Station)

Nicknamed "Kyoto's Kitchen," this 400-meter covered market has over 100 shops. Most items are food, but not everything is packable. Here is what travels well:

| Item | Packability | Price (USD) | Notes |
|------|------------|-------------|-------|
| **Tsukemono** (Japanese pickles) | Good -- vacuum-sealed varieties available | $5-15 | Shibazuke (purple pickles) is Kyoto's signature. Ask for vacuum packs. |
| **Shichimi togarashi** (seven-spice blend) | Excellent | $3-8 | Buy from Uchida-ya or Harima-ya, both on Nishiki |
| **Dried yuba** (tofu skin) | Excellent | $5-10 | Lightweight Kyoto specialty |
| **Matcha powder** (small tins) | Excellent | $8-20 | Smaller selection than Ippodo but convenient |
| **Chirimen sansho** (tiny dried fish with sansho pepper) | Good -- vacuum-sealed | $8-15 | Classic Kyoto souvenir; savory rice topping |
| **Fresh foods** (sashimi, dango, skewers) | Not packable | $2-5 each | Eat on the spot |

### Antiques and Flea Markets

Kyoto's best flea markets run on fixed monthly dates:

| Market | Date | Location | Hours |
|--------|------|----------|-------|
| **Toji Temple Flea Market (Kobo-san)** | 21st of every month | Toji Temple, south of Kyoto Station | 5:00 AM - 4:00 PM |
| **Kitano Tenmangu Flea Market (Tenjin-san)** | 25th of every month | Kitano Tenmangu Shrine, northwest Kyoto | 6:00 AM - 4:00 PM |

**Your dates are Sep 5-9.** Unfortunately, neither the 21st nor the 25th falls within your stay. However, check for these alternatives:

- **Chion-ji Handicraft Market (Hyakumanben)**: Held on the **15th of every month** at Chion-ji Temple. Also does not fall within your dates, but verify whether any special or irregular markets are scheduled for early September 2026.
- **Antique shops along Shinmonzen-dori** (Gion area): Open daily. A street of small antique dealers selling ceramics, scrolls, lacquerware, and woodblock prints. Prices range from $20 to $500+. No haggling here -- prices are fair and fixed.
- **Teramachi Street** (downtown): Antique and craft shops mixed in with modern retail. Good for browsing.

### Fans and Folding Screens

Kyoto is the traditional center for **sensu** (folding fans) and **uchiwa** (flat fans), both of which make beautiful, lightweight gifts.

| Shop | Location | What They Sell | Price Range (USD) |
|------|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Miyawaki Baisen-an** | Tominokoji-Rokkaku, downtown Kyoto | Kyoto's most famous fan shop, est. 1823. Hand-painted silk and paper sensu. | $15-80 for standard; $100-300+ for artisan pieces |
| **Ohnishi Tsune** | Near Kiyomizu-dera | Traditional hand-painted fans, more tourist-oriented | $8-30 |
| Various shops on Ninenzaka/Sannenzaka | Higashiyama district | Mass-produced to mid-range fans, often with floral or ukiyo-e prints | $5-20 |

A quality sensu from Miyawaki Baisen-an is arguably the single best lightweight souvenir from Kyoto.

## Tokyo (Sep 9-15)

With six nights in Tokyo, you have the most shopping time here. Tokyo covers everything from retro gaming to high fashion to stationery to skincare.

### Akihabara

**Location**: Akihabara Station (JR Yamanote Line, Metro Hibiya Line)

The world capital of anime, manga, retro gaming, and electronics subculture.

| Shop | What They Sell | Price Range (USD) | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------------------|-------|
| **Super Potato** | Retro games (NES, SNES, N64, Sega, PS1/PS2), vintage consoles, controllers | $5-100+ | 5 floors of retro gaming. Top floor has a retro arcade. Most stock is Japan-region. |
| **Mandarake Akihabara (Complex)** | Manga, anime figures, doujinshi, vintage toys, cosplay | $3-500+ | 8 floors, each themed. Serious collectors spend hours here. |

| **Yodobashi Camera Akihabara** | Electronics, cameras, appliances, toys | Varies | Massive multi-floor electronics store. Tax-free available. Not always cheapest but huge selection. |

### Vintage and Streetwear

**Shimokitazawa**
- **Location**: Shimokitazawa Station (Keio Inokashira Line or Odakyu Line, ~10 min from Shibuya)
- Tokyo's best neighborhood for vintage and thrift shopping
- Dozens of curated secondhand shops in a walkable area
- Look for: **Flamingo** (well-curated vintage), **Stick Out** (American vintage), **New York Joe Exchange** (huge selection, $5-20 per item)
- Vibe: bohemian, artistic, relaxed. Also great for independent cafes and live music.

**Harajuku / Omotesando**
- **Location**: Harajuku Station (JR Yamanote Line)
- Takeshita Street: fast fashion, quirky accessories, costume-level streetwear. Chaotic and fun. Budget $5-20 per item.
- Cat Street (Ura-Harajuku): higher-end streetwear and independent Japanese brands
- Omotesando: luxury brands in architect-designed flagships (Prada, Dior, etc.)

**Nakano Broadway**
- **Location**: Nakano Station (JR Chuo Line, 5 min from Shinjuku), walk through Nakano Sun Mall
- A multi-floor indoor mall of small collectible shops: vintage toys, anime cels, rare manga, idol goods, watches
- The "underground Akihabara" -- less touristy, often cheaper, and more eclectic
- Multiple **Mandarake** branches inside (the original location)

### Knives (if Not Bought in Osaka)

**Kappabashi Kitchen Street (Kappabashi Dougu-gai)**
- **Location**: Between Ueno and Asakusa (Metro: Tawaramachi Station, Exit 3)
- An 800-meter street of kitchen supply shops: knives, ceramics, plastic food replicas, cookware, restaurant equipment

## Beijing (Sep 15-18)

### Tea

**Maliandao Tea Street**
- **Location**: Maliandao Road, Xuanwu District (south-central Beijing; take a taxi, roughly $5-8 from central areas)
- The largest tea wholesale market in China -- over 900 shops across multiple buildings
- This is where tea shop owners from across the country come to buy stock
- Buy: pu-erh (aged and raw), jasmine tea, tieguanyin, longjing, chrysanthemum tea

| Tea Type | What It Is | Price Range (USD per 100g) | Notes |
|----------|-----------|---------------------------|-------|
| **Pu-erh (shu/ripe)** | Aged, fermented dark tea from Yunnan | $5-50+ | Aged cakes can be very expensive; young shu is affordable |
| **Pu-erh (sheng/raw)** | Unfermented, ages over decades | $8-100+ | Investment tea; ages like wine |
| **Jasmine tea** | Green tea scented with jasmine flowers | $3-15 | Beijing's most popular daily tea |
| **Da Hong Pao** | Prestigious Wuyi rock oolong | $10-60+ | Complex, roasted flavor |

**Haggling tips for Maliandao:**
- The first price quoted will be 2-5x what the vendor expects to receive. Start at 30-40% of the asking price.
- Always taste before buying. Vendors will brew multiple teas for you at no charge.
- Buy in bulk for better prices. Buying 500g+ gets significantly better per-unit pricing.
- Go in the morning when vendors are fresh and more willing to negotiate.
- Bring cash -- some stalls do not accept mobile payment from foreign devices.

### Antiques and Curios

**Panjiayuan Antique Market ("Dirt Market")**
- **Location**: Panjiayuan Bridge, Chaoyang District (Metro: Line 10, Panjiayuan Station, Exit B)
- **Hours**: Open daily, but **weekends (Sat-Sun) are the main event** with 3,000+ vendors. Weekday selection is much smaller.
- **Your dates**: Sep 15 (Mon) through Sep 18 (Thu) -- all weekdays. The market will be open but with fewer vendors. Arrive as early as possible (6:00-7:00 AM) for the best selection.

**What you will find:**

| Category | Examples | Price Range (USD) | Authenticity |
|----------|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| Ceramics and porcelain | Vases, plates, teapots, figurines | $5-200+ | Mix of reproductions and genuine antiques; assume reproduction unless you are an expert |

## General Tips

### Tax-Free Shopping in Japan (Detailed How-To)

Japan's tax-free system exempts foreign tourists from the 10% consumption tax on purchases. This is a meaningful savings, especially on electronics, beauty, and clothing.

**Eligibility:**
- You must be a non-resident of Japan (tourist visa or transit)
- Must show your passport at the tax-free counter
- Your passport must have a valid entry stamp or landing permission sticker

**Two categories of goods:**

| Category | Examples | Minimum Spend | Rules |
|----------|---------|---------------|-------|
| **General goods** | Clothing, electronics, bags, shoes, watches, jewelry | 5,000 yen (~$33) per store per day | Can be used in Japan. No usage restriction. |
| **Consumables** | Food, drinks, cosmetics, medicine, batteries | 5,000 yen (~$33) per store per day | Must be sealed in a special bag at purchase. Do not open until you leave Japan. |

**Combined purchases**: Since April 2024, some stores allow combining general goods and consumables toward the 5,000 yen threshold.

**How to claim:**
1. Make your purchase. Tell the cashier "tax-free please" or look for the "Tax-Free" counter (separate from the regular register in larger stores).
2. Show your passport.
3. The store will process the exemption. Consumables will be sealed in a tamper-evident bag.
4. At departure from Japan, customs may (rarely) check that you still have the sealed goods. Keep them accessible in your carry-on or checked bag.

**Stores that commonly offer tax-free**: Don Quijote, Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Bic Camera, Yodobashi Camera, Uniqlo, Isetan, Takashimaya, Mitsukoshi, Tokyu Hands, Loft, and most major retailers.

### Haggling Guide

| Country | Where to Haggle | Where NOT to Haggle |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| **China** | Street markets, tea markets (Maliandao, Tianshan), Panjiayuan flea market, fabric markets, most non-chain shops | Shopping malls, brand stores, restaurants, convenience stores, supermarkets |
| **Hong Kong** | Ladies Market, Temple Street Night Market, small electronics stalls in Sham Shui Po | Chain stores (Fortress, Broadway), shopping malls, restaurants |
| **Japan** | Nowhere. Haggling is not part of Japanese retail culture and will cause discomfort. The marked price is the price. | Everywhere. |

**Haggling tips for China:**
- Start at 30-40% of the first asking price. The vendor expects to settle at roughly 50-60%.
- Always be polite and smile. Aggressive haggling is counterproductive.
- Walk away slowly if you cannot reach your target price. The vendor will often call you back.
- Use a calculator or phone to show numbers if there is a language barrier.
- Have the exact amount ready in cash once you agree on a price -- do not fumble for change.
- Do not haggle and then not buy. Agreeing on a price is a social contract.
- Shopping in the morning often yields better prices -- vendors consider the first sale of the day lucky.

### Shipping Heavy Purchases Home

If you buy knives, pottery, tea sets, or anything too heavy or bulky for your luggage, shipping is straightforward from both Japan and China.

**Japan Post (from Japan):**
- Available at any post office. Staff are helpful and English forms are available.
- **EMS** (Express Mail Service): fastest, 2-4 days, with tracking. A 2 kg package to the US costs roughly $25-30.
- **Airmail parcel**: slower (5-10 days), cheaper. A 2 kg package costs roughly $15-20.
- **SAL (Surface Air Lifted)**: cheapest international option, 2-3 weeks. A 2 kg package costs roughly $12-15. Currently suspended to some destinations -- verify availability.
- **Sea mail**: cheapest, 1-3 months. Only worthwhile for very heavy, non-urgent shipments.
- Pack items at the post office -- they sell boxes and packing materials.

**China Post (from China):**
- Available at post offices in all cities. English service is limited -- bring a translation app.
- **EMS**: 5-10 days, tracking included. A 2 kg package to the US costs roughly $20-30.
- **Airmail**: 10-20 days. A 2 kg package costs roughly $10-15.
- Surface mail: 1-3 months, $5-10 for 2 kg. Very slow.
- Some tea shops on Maliandao will handle shipping for you at an added cost.

**When shipping makes sense:**
- You have purchased something fragile (pottery, ceramics) and do not want to risk it in luggage
- Your luggage is already full
- You bought something over 3-5 kg that would be burdensome to carry
- For items under 2-3 kg, it is usually cheaper and faster to just put them in your checked bag

### Customs and Duty-Free Limits

These are general guidelines. Check your specific home country's customs website for exact rules before departure.

**US residents returning to the US:**
- **$800 duty-free personal exemption** per person. Items up to this value do not incur duty.
- **Alcohol**: 1 liter duty-free
- **Tobacco**: 200 cigarettes or 100 cigars
- **Prohibited/restricted**: most fresh foods, meats, certain plants, certain animal products. Dried goods (tea, spices, dried noodles) are generally fine.
- Knives are allowed in checked luggage. Never pack knives in carry-on bags.

**What typically passes without issue:**
- Tea, dried spices, packaged snacks, candy, instant noodles
- Knives (in checked luggage)
- Clothing, electronics, pottery, art, paper goods
- Cosmetics and beauty products

**What may be questioned or seized:**
- Dried meats or seafood (varies by country -- check rules for dried scallops, abalone, etc.)
- Fresh fruit or vegetables (almost universally prohibited)
- Soil or plants
- Counterfeit goods (fake luxury brands can be confiscated)

### Packing Strategy

- **Leave space**: Do not fill your suitcase to 100% on the way out. Plan for at least 5-10 kg of shopping weight and some extra volume.
- **Bring a foldable duffel bag**: A packable bag like a Muji or Uniqlo foldable duffel weighs almost nothing and gives you an entire extra bag for the return trip. You can also buy one cheaply in Japan.
- **Compression packing cubes**: Keep clothes compressed to free up space for souvenirs.
- **Wrap fragile items**: Bring a few sheets of bubble wrap or buy it at post offices. Clothing also works as padding around pottery or bottles.
- **Weight limits**: Most airlines allow 23 kg (50 lbs) per checked bag on international flights. Budget airlines in Asia may charge for checked bags -- verify before you fly.
- **Knife transport**: Always pack knives in checked luggage, wrapped securely in the cardboard sheath or box they came in, then rolled in clothing.

### Scam Awareness

**Tea Ceremony / Tea House Scam (Shanghai and Beijing)**
- Two or three friendly young locals approach you near tourist areas (the Bund, Nanjing Road, Tiananmen, Wangfujing) and strike up conversation in good English
- They suggest visiting a "traditional tea house" or "art exhibition" together
- At the tea house, you are served tea in a private room. The bill arrives: $50-200+ per person for a few cups of tea
- The "friends" pretend to be shocked but pay their share. You are pressured to pay yours. The "friends" and the tea house are in on it together.
- **How to avoid**: Never follow strangers to a tea house, art show, or restaurant. If approached, politely decline and walk away. This scam specifically targets solo travelers and couples.

**"Student Art" Scam (Shanghai and Beijing)**
- "Art students" invite you to see their "graduation exhibition" nearby
- The "gallery" is a high-pressure sales room where you are pushed to buy overpriced, mass-produced art
- **How to avoid**: Politely decline any invitation from strangers to visit an art show or gallery

**Fake Goods Markets**
- Markets in China (and to a lesser extent Hong Kong) may sell counterfeit luxury goods (bags, watches, clothing)
- Buying counterfeits is illegal in most countries, and customs officers can seize them
- Beyond legality, quality is unpredictable and you have no recourse
- **If approached by touts** offering to take you to a "special room" with luxury goods, walk away

**Taxi Meter Scams (China)**
- Some taxi drivers may claim the meter is "broken" and quote a flat fare (always inflated)
- **Solution**: Only take taxis that use the meter, or use Didi ride-hailing (price set by app). In Beijing and Shanghai, licensed taxis are required by law to use meters.

**General Rules:**
- If a deal seems too good to be true, it is
- Never let a stranger choose the restaurant, bar, or shop
- Use Didi instead of hailing taxis on the street in China
- Keep your phone and wallet secure in crowded markets
- Photograph receipts for expensive purchases (useful for customs and returns)
