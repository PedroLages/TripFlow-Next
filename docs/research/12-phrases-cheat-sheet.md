# Useful Phrases Cheat Sheet

**Three languages for six cities: Mandarin Chinese (Shanghai, Beijing), Cantonese (Hong Kong), and Japanese (Osaka, Kyoto, Tokyo).**

Each phrase includes the local script and a romanization you can read aloud. When in doubt, pointing at the phrase on your phone screen works surprisingly well.

## Cantonese (Hong Kong)

Hong Kong's primary language is Cantonese, which is mutually unintelligible with Mandarin despite sharing the same written characters (traditional characters in HK, simplified on the mainland). The good news: most Hong Kong residents under 50 understand at least some Mandarin, and English is widely spoken in tourist and business areas. Jyutping romanization is provided below -- the numbers indicate tones.

**Key difference from Mandarin:** Common words like "thank you" and "excuse me" are completely different. Do not assume Mandarin phrases will be understood in casual Cantonese conversation.

### Basics

| English | Chinese (Traditional) | Jyutping |
|---|---|---|
| Hello | 你好 | nei5 hou2 |
| Thank you (for a gift/favor) | 多謝 | do1 ze6 |
| Thank you (for a service) | 唔該 | m4 goi1 |
| Sorry / Excuse me | 對唔住 / 唔該 | deoi3 m4 zyu6 / m4 goi1 |
| Yes | 係 | hai6 |
| No | 唔係 | m4 hai6 |
| I don't understand | 我聽唔明 | ngo5 teng1 m4 ming4 |

| Goodbye | 再見 / 拜拜 | zoi3 gin3 / baai1 baai3 |

**Note:** "m4 goi1" (唔該) is the most useful word in Hong Kong. It means "excuse me," "please," and "thank you" (for services like a waiter bringing food). Use it liberally.

## Japanese (Osaka, Kyoto, Tokyo)

Japanese uses three scripts: hiragana, katakana (for foreign words), and kanji (Chinese characters). Romaji romanization is provided below. Japanese is relatively easy to pronounce -- every syllable gets equal stress, and vowels are consistent (a=ah, i=ee, u=oo, e=eh, o=oh).

### Basics

| English | Japanese | Romaji |
|---|---|---|
| Hello (general) | こんにちは | konnichiwa |
| Good morning | おはようございます | ohayou gozaimasu |
| Good evening | こんばんは | konbanwa |
| Thank you | ありがとうございます | arigatou gozaimasu |
| Thank you (casual) | ありがとう | arigatou |
| Sorry / Excuse me | すみません | sumimasen |
| I'm sorry (apology) | ごめんなさい | gomen nasai |
| Yes | はい | hai |
| No | いいえ | iie |
| I don't understand | わかりません | wakarimasen |
| Do you speak English? | 英語を話せますか？ | eigo o hanasemasu ka? |
| Please (requesting) | お願いします | onegaishimasu |
| Goodbye | さようなら | sayounara |
| See you / Bye (casual) | じゃあね / バイバイ | jaa ne / bai bai |

**Note:** "Sumimasen" is the Swiss army knife of Japanese. It means "excuse me," "I'm sorry," and even "thank you" (for trouble someone took on your behalf). Use it constantly.

### Restaurant-Specific

**Before and after eating:**

| English | Japanese | Romaji |
|---|---|---|
| (Said before eating) | いただきます | itadakimasu |
| (Said after eating) | ごちそうさまでした | gochisousama deshita |

"Itadakimasu" (literally "I humbly receive") is said before every meal. "Gochisousama deshita" ("it was a feast") is said when finished. Both are expected and appreciated.

### Osaka Dialect (Osaka-ben)

Osaka has its own dialect that locals use with pride. You will hear these constantly in Osaka and occasionally in Kyoto. Using a few Osaka-ben phrases will delight locals.

| Standard Japanese | Osaka-ben | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| ありがとう | おおきに | ookini | Thank you |
| いらっしゃいませ | まいど | maido | Welcome (at shops) |
| だめ | あかん | akan | No good / No way |
| 本当？ | ほんま？ | honma? | Really? |
| なぜ / 何で | なんでやねん | nandeyanen | Why! / What the heck! |
| とても | めっちゃ | meccha | Very / Super |
| 違う | ちゃう | chau | Different / Wrong |
| おもしろい | おもろい | omoroi | Funny / Interesting |

"Nandeyanen" is the classic Osaka comedic retort -- roughly "what are you even saying!" It is the signature phrase of Osaka humor. Using it at the right moment will earn instant respect.

## Cultural Notes

### Bowing in Japan

Bowing is automatic in Japan -- you will see it everywhere. As a tourist, a slight nod of the head (about 15 degrees) is perfectly sufficient and appreciated for casual thanks and greetings. A deeper bow (30 degrees) is appropriate when expressing genuine gratitude or apology. You do not need to match the depth or duration of bows from service staff; they are trained to bow deeply as part of their role. When in doubt, a polite head nod works.

### Volume and Personal Space

Japan is notably quiet in public spaces. Keep your voice low on trains, in elevators, and in restaurants. Phone calls on public transit are considered rude -- text instead. In contrast, China (both Shanghai and Beijing) operates at a higher volume. Restaurants are lively and loud, and speaking up to get a waiter's attention is normal and expected, not rude. Hong Kong falls somewhere in between. Adjust your volume to match your surroundings.

### Tipping

**Do not tip anywhere on this trip.** Tipping is not customary in China, Hong Kong, or Japan. In Japan, it can cause genuine confusion or even offense -- staff may chase you down to return the money, thinking you forgot it. Some upscale Hong Kong restaurants add a 10% service charge automatically, but that is built into the bill, not a tip you leave.

### Receiving Items in Japan

When receiving change, a credit card, a business card, or any item handed to you in Japan, accept it with both hands. This is a basic gesture of respect. When handing your credit card or IC card to someone, present it with both hands as well. This applies in shops, restaurants, hotels, and any service interaction.

### Chopstick Etiquette (All Three Regions)

- Never stick chopsticks upright in a bowl of rice. This resembles incense sticks at a funeral and is considered very bad luck across all three cultures.
- Do not pass food directly from your chopsticks to someone else's chopsticks. This also mimics a funeral rite. Place the food on their plate instead.
- Do not point at people or things with your chopsticks.
- In Japan, when taking food from a shared plate, use the reverse (thick) end of your chopsticks if serving utensils are not provided.

### Other Practical Points

- **China -- digital payments:** In Shanghai and Beijing, nearly everything runs on WeChat Pay and Alipay. Cash can be difficult to use at smaller vendors. As a foreign tourist, you can now link an international credit card to Alipay for payments. Set this up before arrival.
- **Hong Kong -- Octopus card:** Get an Octopus card at the airport. It works on the MTR, buses, ferries, 7-Eleven, and many restaurants. It is the easiest way to pay for almost everything.
- **Japan -- cash still matters:** Despite increasing card acceptance, many smaller restaurants, temples, and local shops in Japan are cash only. Always carry at least 10,000 yen in cash.
- **Queuing:** Japan and Hong Kong take orderly queuing seriously. China is more fluid -- do not be offended by what feels like cutting in line; it is simply a different cultural norm around personal space and queuing.
